Automated Pedestrian Crosswalk Warning System

Sensor-based safety system that detects pedestrians and cyclists approaching or entering a crosswalk, activating yellow or red warning lights to alert drivers in low-visibility areas and improve intersection safety.

Fall 2019 | Group project

GEEN 1400 | Freshman Engineering Projects

Overview

Pedestrian safety remains a persistent challenge at intersections without traffic signals, where drivers often fail to notice pedestrians in time to yield. This project addressed that problem through the design and prototyping of an Automated Pedestrian Crosswalk Warning System, intended to increase driver awareness using clear visual cues when pedestrians approach or enter a crosswalk.

Automated Pedestrian Crosswalk Warning System Demonstration

The system was designed around a simple two-state warning concept. A yellow warning light activates when a pedestrian approaches the crosswalk, while a red warning light activates once a pedestrian is actively crossing. This distinction allows drivers to anticipate pedestrian movement rather than reacting at the last moment.

Warning Light Explanation

Pedestrian detection was achieved using a layered sensing approach. An ultrasonic sensor detected pedestrians approaching the crosswalk, while a passive infrared (PIR) sensor confirmed when a pedestrian entered the crossing zone. Timers and logic thresholds were implemented to reduce false activations and ensure signals remained active for the duration of a crossing.

A functional prototype was constructed using off-the-shelf components, with an emphasis on affordability and feasibility. The total prototype cost was approximately $105, demonstrating that meaningful safety improvements could be achieved without expensive infrastructure changes.

The system was tested in real-world conditions across multiple pedestrian movement types, including walking, running, biking, and skateboarding. Testing showed approximately 80‚Äì90% detection accuracy, validating the sensing approach while highlighting opportunities for improvement through higher-grade sensors and improved placement.

While the prototype was not intended for permanent deployment, the project emphasized realistic engineering tradeoffs, including system reliability, human interpretation of signals, and potential liability concerns. Future improvements would focus on sensor redundancy, weatherproofing, and integration into existing urban infrastructure.
